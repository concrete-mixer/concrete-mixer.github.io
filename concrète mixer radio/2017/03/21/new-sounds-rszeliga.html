Concrète Mixer - shredded field recording ambience

New sounds by Rob Szeliga added to Concrète Mixer radio

March 21, 2017

Our second sound donor (praise him with great praise!) is Rob Szeliga, a UK-based sound designer, and he’s provided a nice range of diverse recordings.

Contributions

Prayers at Osu Kannon Temple

This recording features a bit of tranquil bonging and chanting from a Buddhist temple in Japan. I’ve spent a bit of time in a few Japanese temples myself (a sign, I fear, of a misspent youth), so this recording brings back some memories of stone gardens, exquisite lawns, golden statues and, weirdly, eagles flying over head.

Incidentally, the base CM sound library includes a recording I did at the Temple of Ten Thousand Buddhas in Sha Tin, Hong Kong. That lacks the tranquility of this recording, largely because it was Chinese New Year and it was crowded with people casting fortune sticks to see if they’d get rich that year.

Wind through stone wall

This excellent recording really transports you to a rather wind day in the English countryside. Makes you want to put on another layer or two of clothing. Brr!

The horns of India

Firstly, the chaotic blarings of the bells are great - nice rich sound source that - but Rob did a bit of granular synthesis to freeze the sounds in time and stretch them out into a sort of brief illbient track. I’ve excised the signal processing as I want the recordings used in Concrete Mixer to be a document of something that happened somewhere rather than an act of post-processing (in theory CM is supposed to be doing that).

That said, it’s a nice piece of filtration and I feel a bit bad about the removal. Sorry Rob!

Midday at the Foundling Museum

It’s hard to imagine how midday could be signified with more gravitas than this clock manages. It also clamours well with the other bells recorded in the CM collection. By our sonorousnesses combined!

Underground Screech

There have been times when I’ve been on the London Underground where I’ve been a little concerned I’d never made it back to the outside world. It’s certainly reassuring to hear a train in motion. If the photo’s any guide this would be a <anorak>train on one of the deep lines (eg Central, Victoria, Picadilly)</anorak>.

Traction Engines

I come from a rather young country, with few traction engines about, but I did happen to see one once (somewhat anachronistically, on display at an art deco festival). It sort of puffed and bubbled water and a long belt wobbled around. And so does this recording.

Thanks Rob!
